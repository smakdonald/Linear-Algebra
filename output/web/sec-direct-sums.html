    Section 4.1 Direct Sum of Vector Spaces

    We introduce now two constructions of new vector spaces from old.

    Subsection Internal direct sum

    Let's first notice that we can add and scale subspaces of a vector space just like we add and scale vectors. To add two subspaces we will in fact adding together elements from the subspaces in all possible ways. Our notation is as follows.
    If \(A,B\) are subsets of a vector space \(V\) over \(\F\) and \(\l \in \F\) we define
    \begin{equation*} A+B = \setof{\vv{a}+\b}{\vv{a}\in A, \b\in B} \qquad \l A = \setof{\l \vv{a}}{\vv{a}\in A}. \end{equation*}
    We make corresponding definitions for more complicated expressions such as \(A+B+(-12)C\text{.}\)
    If \(U\) and \(V\) are subspaces of a vector space \(W\) and \(\l \in \F\) then \(U+V\) and \(\l U\) are subspaces of \(W\text{.}\)
    That \(U+V\) is a subspace was proven on homework. Since \(U\) is a subspace \(\l U=U\) if \(\l\neq 0\) and \(0U=\{\0_W\}\text{.}\) In either case \(\l U\) is a subspace of \(W\text{.}\)
    By induction, it follows that any linear combination of subspaces of \(W\) is a subspace of \(W\text{.}\)
    Recall that we introduced the idea of vectors \(\v_1, \ldots, \v_n\) being linearly independent to model the fact that ever vector isn \(\linspan\{\v_1, \ldots, \v_n\}\) can we written uniquely as a linear combination of \(\v_1, \ldots, \v_n\text{.}\) Now we want to define a notion similar to linear independence for subspaces.
    If \(U\) and \(V\) are subspaces of a vector space \(W\) we say that \(U+V\) is an internal direct sum and we denote \(U\oplus V=U+V\) if every vector \(\w\in U+V\) can be written uniquely as \(\w=\u+\v\) with \(\u\in U\) and \(\v\in V\text{.}\)
    More generally, if \(V_1,\ldots, V_n\) are subspaces of a vector space \(W\) we say that \(V_1+\cdots+ V_n\) is an internal direct sum and we denote it \(V_1\oplus \cdots \oplus V_n\) if every vector \(\w\in V_1+\cdots+ V_n\) can be written uniquely as \(\w=\v_1+\cdots+\v_n\) with \(\v_i\in V_i\text{.}\)
    We can even have infinite direct sums: suppose that \((V_i)_{i\in I}\) is a family of subspaces of a vector space \(W\) and any vector \(\w=\v_1+\cdots+\v_n\) with \(\v_i\in V_i\) can be written uniquely in this form we say we have a direct sum \(\bigoplus_{i\in I} V_i\text{.}\)
    Let \(W=\R^3\) and let \(U=\{(x,y,0)\mid x,y\in \R\}\) and \(V=\{(0,0,0,z)\mid z\in \R\}\text{.}\) Then \(\R^3=U\oplus V\text{.}\)
    Let \(W=M_{n\times n} (\R)\) and let \(U\) and \(V\) be the subspaces of symmetric and antisymmetric matrices respectively. In detail \(A\in U\) if and only if \(A=A^T\) and \(A\in V\) if and only if \(A=-A^T\text{,}\) where \(T\) denotes transpose. Then \(W=U\oplus V\) as we can write
    \begin{equation*} A=\frac{1}{2}(A+A^T) +\frac{1}{2}(A-A^T) \end{equation*}
    and moreover if \(A=B+C\) with \(B\in U\) and \(C\in V\) then we have \(A^T=B^T+C^T=B-C\text{.}\) From
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} A &=B+C\\ A^T&=B-C \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    we obtain \(B=\frac{1}{2}(A+A^T)\) and \(C=\frac{1}{2}(A-A^T)\text{.}\)
    Let \(T_i\) be a one-dimensional vector space over \(\R\) whose basis vector we call \(X^i\text{.}\) In short \(T_i=\R X^i\text{.}\) Then we have
    \begin{equation*} \R[X] = \Dsum_{i=0}^\infty T_i. \end{equation*}
    Let \(U\) and \(V\) be subspaces of a vector space \(W\text{.}\) Then \(U+V\) is a direct sum if and only if \(U\cap V=\{\0_W\}\text{.}\)
    Suppose \(U+V\) is a direct sum and let \(\w\in U\cap V\text{.}\) Then \(\w=\0+\w=\w+\0\) are two different ways of writing \(\w\) as a sum of a vector in \(U\) and a vector in \(V\) unless \(\w=\0\text{.}\)
    Suppose \(U\cap V=\{\0_W\}\text{.}\) If \(\u+\v=\u'+\v'\) with \(\u,\u'\in U\) and \(\v, \v'\in V\) then \(\u-\u'=\v'-\v\text{.}\) The left hand side is in \(U\) and the right hand side is in \(V\text{.}\) So we must have \(\u-\u'=\v'-\v\in U\cap V\text{,}\) therefore \(\u-\u'=\v'-\v=\0\text{.}\) So \(\u=\u'\) and \(\v=\v'\text{.}\)
    One place where we (silently) thought about an internal direct sum was in the proof of [cross-reference to target(s) "thm:ranknullity" missing or not unique]. Looking at that proof we see that we really showed that if \(T:V \to W\) then \(V\) is the internal direct sum of \(\ker(T)\) and \(\linspan(\set{\vs[\ell]})\text{.}\)
    Let \(U\) and \(V\) be subspaces of a vector space \(W\text{.}\) If \(B\) is a basis for \(U\) and \(B'\) is a basis for \(V\) then \(B\cup B'\) is a basis for \(U\oplus V\text{.}\) In particular,
    \begin{equation*} \dim U\oplus V=\dim U+\dim V. \end{equation*}
    The last part follows from the formula \(\dim U + V=\dim U+\dim V-\dim (U\cap V)\) proven on the homework and which gives \(U\cap V=\{\0\}\) so that \(\dim (U\cap V)=0\text{.}\)
    Let \(U\) is a subspace of a vector space \(W\) then there exists a subspace \(V\) of \(W\) so that \(U\oplus V=W\text{.}\)
    Let \(B_U\) be a basis for \(U\text{.}\) Since \(B_U\) is an independent set, there exists a basis \(B\) of \(W\) so that \(B_u\subseteq B\text{.}\) Set \(B_V=B\setminus B_U\) and \(V=\linspan{B_V}\text{.}\) Then \(V\) is a subspce of \(W\text{.}\)
    Any \(\w\in W\) can be written as \(\w=\l_1\b_1+\cdots+\l_n\b_n\) for some \(\l_1,\ldots, \l_n\in \F\) and \(\b_1,\ldots, \b_n\in B\text{.}\) After renumbering we may assume \(\b_1,\ldots, b_t\in B_U\) and \(\b_{t+1},\ldots, b_n\in B_V\) so that \(\w=\u+\v\) where \(\u=\l_1\b_1+\cdots+\l_t\b_t\in U\) and \(\v=\l_1\b_{t+1}+\cdots+\l_n\b_n\in V\text{.}\) We have shown \(W=U+V\text{.}\)
    Now suppose \(\w\in U\cap V\text{.}\) Then \(\w=\l_1\b_1+\cdots+\l_t\b_t\) for some \(\b_1,\ldots, b_t\in B_U\) and also \(\w=\l_1\b_{t+1}+\cdots+\l_n\b_n\) for some \(\b_{t+1},\ldots, b_n\in B_V\text{.}\) Therefore we have
    \begin{equation*} \l_1\b_1+\cdots+\l_t\b_t-\l_{t+1}\b_{t+1}-\cdots -\l_n\b_n=\0 \end{equation*}
    with \(\b_1,\ldots, \b_n\in B\) distinct. Since \(B\) is linearly independent we conclude \(\l_i=0\) for all \(1\leq i\leq n\) whence \(\w=\0\text{.}\) We have shown that \(U\cap V=\{\0\}\) and it follows from that \(W=U\oplus V\text{.}\)
    The subspace \(V\) in the above theorem is not unique. For example set \(U=\{(x,0,0)\mid x\in \R\}\subseteq W=\R^3\text{.}\) Then both \(V=\{(0,y,z)\mid y,z\in \R\}\) and \(V'=\{(x,x,z)\mid x,z\in \R\}\) satisfy \(U\oplus V=U\oplus V'=\R^3\text{.}\)

    Subsection External direct sum and direct product

    Suppose that \((V_i)_{i\in I}\) is a family of vector spaces over a field \(\F\text{.}\) We would like to exhibit a vector space \(W\) that contains (copies of) each of the \(V_i\) without assuming that they are subspaces of the same vector field to begin with. Since \(W\)contains the \(V_i\) it must of course also contain linear combinations of vector from the \(V_i\text{.}\) We will arrange that these linear combinations cannot “accidentally” produce any sort of cancellation. In particular for instance we want it to be the case that if \(\v\in V_i\) and \(\w\in V_j\) then the only way to have \(\v+\w=\0\) is for each of \(\v\) and \(\w\) to be \(\0\text{.}\)
    We define
    \begin{equation*} \Dsum_{i\in I} V_i = \setof{(\v_i)_{i\in I}}{\text{$\forall i\in I,\v_i\in V_i$ and only finitely many of the $\v_i$ are non-zero}}. \end{equation*}
    This is the external direct sum of the \(V_i\text{.}\) The operations are performed coordinate-wise:
    \begin{equation*} (\v_i)_{i\in I}+(\w_i)_{i\in I} = (\v_i+\w_i)_{i\in I}, \end{equation*}
    and similarly for scalar multiplication by \(\l\in \F\)
    \begin{equation*} \l(\v_i)_{i\in I}= (\l \v_i)_{i\in I}, \end{equation*}
    It is easy to check that with these operations \(\Dsum_{i\in I} V_i\) is a vector space.
    It is sometimes desirable to eliminate the restriction that only finitely many of the \(\v_i\) are non-zero. Then we get the direct product.
    \begin{equation*} \prod_{i\in I} V_i = \setof{(\v_i)_{i\in I}}{\forall i\in I,\v_i\in V_i}. \end{equation*}
    In both \(\Dsum_{i\in I} V_i\) and \(\prod_{i\in I} V_i\) there is a copy of \(V_i\text{.}\) We can identify \(V_i\) with the set of vectors that are \(\0\) everywhere except in the \(i^{\text{th}}\) component. The direct sum is spanned by the union of these copies of the \(V_i\text{,}\) but (at least when \(I\) is infinite) the direct product is not.
    In the cases where our two definitions of direct sum both apply (i.e., when considering \(U+W\) where \(U,W\) are subspaces of \(V\)) the two definitions agree. In particular with the external direct sum on the left and the internal direct sum on the right the following map is an isomorphism:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} S \colon U\dsum W &\to U\dsum W \\ (\u,\w) &\mapsto \u+\w \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    The fact that \(\range(S)\) is all of \(U+W\) is clear. To prove that \(\ker(S)=\set{(\0,\0)}\) consider a pair \((\u,\w)\in U \dsum W\) such that \(\u+\w=\0\text{.}\) We need to show that \(\u=\w=\0\text{.}\) Well, since \(\u=-\w\) we have \(\u\in U\cap W=\set{\0}\) so \(\u=0\) and hence \(\w=\0\text{.}\)
