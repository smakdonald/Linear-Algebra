    Section 5.7 Adjoints

    Suppose \(V,W\) are real vector spaces each equipped with an inner product, and that \(T\in L(V,W)\text{.}\) We define the adjoint of \(T\text{,}\) written \(T^*:W\to V\) as follows. Given a vector \(\w\in W\) we define a linear map in \(V\dual\) by
    \begin{equation*} \v \mapsto \ip{T(\v),\w}. \end{equation*}
    This is clearly linear (once we check that we can in fact compute \(\ip{T(\v),\w}\) since \(T(\v)\in W\)). Therefore, by the Riesz Representation Theorem, there is a unique vector, that we denote by \(T^*(\w)\text{,}\) with the property that for all \(\v\in V\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \ip{T(\v),\w} = \ip{\v,T^*(\w)} \end{equation*}
    The next lemma verifies that \(T^*:W\to V\) is linear.
    Given \(T:V\to W\) between finite dimensional Euclidean spaces the map \(T^*:W\to V\) is linear.
    We need to check that for \(\w,\w'\in W\) and \(\l,\mu\in \R\) the vector \(\l T^*(\w)+\mu T^*(\w')\) has the property that for for all \(\v\in V\)
    \begin{equation*} \ip{T(\v),\l\w+\mu\w'} = \ip{\v,\l T^*(\w)+\mu T^*(\w')}. \end{equation*}
    To verify this we compute as follows:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \ip{T(\v),\l\w+\mu\w'} &= \l\ip{T(\v),\w} + \mu\ip{T(\v),\w'} \\ &= \l\ip{\v,T^*(\w)} + \mu\ip{\v,T^*(\w')} \\ &= \ip{\v,\l T^*(\w)+\mu T^*(\w')}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    The second equality used the definition of \(T^*(\w)\) and \(T^*(\w')\text{.}\)
    Consider the subspace \(V =\linspan\set{\sin(kx),\cos(kx)\mid 0\leq k\leq n} \of C[0,2\pi]\text{.}\) Let the linear map \(D:V\to V\) be the differentiation map \(D(f) = f'\text{.}\) I claim that \(D^*:V\to V\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} D^* = -D. \end{equation*}
    To prove this we have to show that for all \(f\in F_n\) and \(g\in F_n\) we have \(\ip{D(f),g} = \ip{f,-D(g)}\text{.}\) This is simply integration by parts:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \ip{D(f),g} &= \definite f'(x) g(x) dx0{2\pi} \\ &= \left. f(x) g(x) \vphantom{\Big(}\right|_0^{2\pi} - \definite f(x) g'(x) dx0{2\pi} \\ &= -\ip{f,D(g)} = \ip{f,-D(g)}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    For a matrix \(A\) with entries in \(\C\) we define \(A^*=(\overline{A})^T\) to be the conjugate-transpose of \(A\text{.}\) Specifically, if \(A=[a_{ij}]\) then \(A^*=[\overline{a_{ji}}]\text{.}\) Transposeing commutes with conjugation so we could also write \(A^*=(\overline{A^T})\text{.}\)
    If the entries of \(A\) are in \(\R\) then \(A^*=A^T\text{.}\)
    Consider the vector spaces \(\C^n\) and \(\C^m\) with their standard inner product . The adjoint of the linear map \(M_A:\C^n\to \C^m, M_A(\x)=A\x\) is the linear map \(M_{A^*}:\C^m\to \C^n, M_{A^*}:(\x)=A^*\x\text{.}\) To see this note that for \(\v\in \C^n\) and \(\w\in \C^m\)
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \ip{M_A(\v),\w} &= \ip{A\v,\w} \\ &= (A\v)\trans \overline{ \w} \\ &= \v\trans A\trans \overline{ \w}\\ &= \v\trans \overline{\overline{A\trans}\w}\\ &= \ip{\v,\overline{A\trans} \w}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    We will prove a version of the Rank-Nullity formula that gives, for inner product spaces, an even clearer picture of the situation.
    If \(V,W\) are inner product spaces and \(T\in L(V,W)\) then \(T^{**} = T\text{.}\)
    If \(\v\in V\) then the vector \(T^{**}\) is the unique vector in \(W\) satisfying
    \begin{equation*} \ip{T^*(\w),\v} = \ip{\w,T^{**} (\v)} \end{equation*}
    for all \(\w\in W\text{.}\) We'll show that \(T(\v)\) has this property; for all \(\w\in W\)
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \ip{T^*(\w),\v} &=\overline{ \ip{\v,T^*(\w)}} \\ &=\overline{ \ip{T(\v),\w}} \\ &= \overline{ \overline{\ip{\w,T(\v)}}}=\ip{\w,T(\v)}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Thus \(T^{**} (\v)=T(\v)\) for all \(\v\in V\text{.}\)
    If \(\v,\w\in V\text{,}\) an inner product space, then we say that two subspaces \(U,W\) of \(V\) are orthogonal if \(\u,\w\) are orthogonal for all \(\u\in U\text{,}\) \(\w\in W\text{.}\) If \(V=U\dsum W\) and \(U,W\) are orthogonal then we write
    \begin{equation*} V = U \odsum W. \qedhere \end{equation*}
    If \(V,W\) are finite dimensional inner product spaces and \(T\in L(V,W)\) then
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} V &= \ker(T) \odsum \range(T^*) \\ W &= \range(T) \odsum \ker(T^*). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Moreover \(\rank(T) = \rank(T^*)\text{.}\)
    First note that by [cross-reference to target(s) "lem:transtrans" missing or not unique] we need prove only one of the two equalities, since the second is simply what the first says about \(T^*\text{.}\) We start by showing that \(\ker(T)\) is orthogonal to \(\range(T^*)\text{,}\) and that therefore in particular their intersection is \(\set{\0}\text{.}\) Take \(\u\in \ker(T)\) and \(\v=T^*(\w)\in \range(T^*)\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} \ip{\u,\v} = \ip{\u,T^*(\w)} = \ip{T(\u),\w} = \ip{\0,\w} = 0. \end{equation*}
    To prove that \(\ker(T)+\range(T^*)\) is all of \(V\) we're going to give a dimension argument and use that \(\rank(T) = \rank(T^*)\text{,}\) or in other words that \(\dim(\range(T)) = \dim(\range(T^*))\text{.}\) We'll start by proving that \(\rank(T^*)\le \rank(T)\text{.}\) Consider then the linear map
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \tilde{T}\colon \range(T^*) &\to \range(T) \\ \v &\mapsto T(\v) \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    This map has trivial kernel, exactly by the argument above: a vector \(\v\) that was in \(\range(T^*)\) and had \(T(\v)=\0\) would be in \(\ker(T)\cap \range(T^*)=\set{\0}\text{.}\) By [cross-reference to target(s) "thm:dim_monotone" missing or not unique] we get \(\dim(\range(T^*))\le \dim\range(T)\text{.}\) Now using [cross-reference to target(s) "lem:transtrans" missing or not unique] again we have \(\rank(T)=\rank(T^{**} )\le \rank(T^*)\text{.}\) This tells us that \(\ker(T) \odsum \range(T^*)\) is a subspace of \(V\) having dimension \(\dim(V)\text{,}\) so the two spaces are equal.
    Suppose that \(U\) is a subspace of an inner product space \(V\) and we consider the inclusion map
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} T \colon U &\to V \\ \u &\mapsto \u. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    What is \(T^*\text{?}\) It is the orthogonal projection onto \(U\text{.}\) Since \(V = U\odsum U\perp\) we can represent any vector \(\v\in V\) uniquely as \(\v=\u+\w\) where \(\u\in U\) and \(\w\in U\perp\text{.}\) The projection map is the map \(P:V\to U\) sending \(\v\) to \(\u\text{.}\) To prove that \(T^* = P\) consider \(\u'\in U\) and \(\v\in V\) with \(\v=\u+\w\) as before. Thus \(T(\u')=\u'\) and \(P(\v)=\u\text{.}\) We need to show that \(\ip{T(\u'),\v} = \ip{\u',P(\v)}\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \ip{T(\u'),\v} = \ip{\u',\u+\w} = \ip{\u',\u} + \ip{\u',\w} = \ip{\u',\u} = \ip{\u',P(\v)}, \end{equation*}
    as required.
    Let \(T:V\to W\) be an injective linear map on a finite dimensional inner product space. Then the map \(T^* T:V\to V\) is bijective.
    To show the map is bijective it suffices to show it is injective, that is, \(\ker(T^* T)=\set{\0}\text{.}\) Suppose that \(T^* T(\v)=\0\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} \ip{T(\v),T(\v)} = \ip{\v,T^* T(\v)} = \ip{\v,\0} =0, \end{equation*}
    so \(T(\v)=0\text{.}\) Since \(T\) is injective we deduce that \(\v=\0\text{,}\) so we're done.
    Returning to our example of fitting a straight line to our set \(\set{(x_i,y_i)}_1^N\) of points we see that \(\vv{p}=\proj_{\range(M)}\vv{y}\) is the closest point in \(\range(M)\) to \(\vv{y}\text{.}\) Thus to solve for \(\alpha\) and \(B\) we should solve \(M\col{\alpha \\\beta } = \vv{p}\text{.}\)
    The solution to the least squares problem in is
    \begin{equation*} \col{\alpha \\ \beta} = (M\trans M)\inv M\trans \vv{y}. \end{equation*}
    Let \(T:V\to V\) be the linear map given by \(T(\v)=M\v\text{.}\)
    The final piece of the puzzle is the fact that by [cross-reference to target(s) "thm:four_subspaces" missing or not unique] we know exactly what \(\range(T)\perp\) is: it's just \(\ker(T^*)\) and over the real numbers \(T^*(\w)=M^T\w\text{.}\) Thus we can compute as follows using that \(\vv{y}=\vv{p}+\vv{n}\) with \(\vv{n}\in \range(T)\perp\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} M\col{A\\B} &= \vv{p}\\ &= \vv{y}-\vv{n}\\ M\trans M \col{A\\B} &= M\trans (\vv{y}-\vv{n}) \\ &= M\trans\vv{y}. \intertext{By the previous lemma $M^TM$ is invertible so there is a unique solution} \col{A\\B} &= (M\trans M)\inv M\trans \vv{y}\\ \vv{p}= M\col{A\\B} &= M (M\trans M)\inv M\trans \vv{y}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
