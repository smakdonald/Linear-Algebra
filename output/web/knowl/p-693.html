If
\begin{equation*} A = \mat[2&1\\4&-1] \end{equation*}
then the map \(T=M_A:\R^2\to \R^2\) has an eigenvector of eigenvalue \(\l\) exactly if the matrix
\begin{equation*} A - \l I = \mat[2-\l&1\\4&-1-\l] \end{equation*}
has rank less than \(2\text{.}\) We have a quick way of determining (this is a pun) whether the matrix \(\mat[a&b\\c&d]\) has rank less than \(2\text{.}\) This happens precisely when \(ad-bc=0\text{.}\) Thus \(\l\) is an eigenvalue of \(T\) exactly if
\begin{equation*} (2-\l)(-1-\l) - 4 = \l^2 - \l -6 = (\l-3)(\l+2) = 0. \end{equation*}
Thus the eigenvalues of \(T\) are precisely \(3,-2\text{.}\) To find the corresponding eigenvectors we need to find \(\ker(T-\l I)\) for each of \(\l=3,-2\text{.}\) We have
\begin{equation*} \ker(A-3I) = \ker\mat[-1&1\\4&-4] = \linspan\set{\col{1\\1}} \qquad \ker(A+2I) = \ker\mat[4&1\\4&1] = \linspan\set{\col{1\\-4}}. \end{equation*}
If we choose the basis \(B=\{\v,\w\}\) where \(\v=\col{1\\1}\) and \(\w=\col{1\\-4}\) then the matrices of \(T\) and \(T^n\) with respect to this basis are
\begin{equation*} [T]_B^B= \mat[3&0\\0&-2] \qquad \text{and} \qquad [T^n]_B^B= \mat[3^n&0\\0&(-2)^n] \end{equation*}
respectively.
