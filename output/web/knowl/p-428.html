This procedure of completing the square allows us to express any quadratic function on \(\R^n\) as a linear combination of squares of linear expressions. What this amounts to is that if we had taken a different basis \(\set{\v_1,\v_2,\v_3}\) for \(\R^3\) with the property that
\begin{equation*} (x,y,z) = (x+2y-6z) \v_1 + (y+4z) \v_2 + z \v_3, \end{equation*}
then that basis 14  would have the property that \(Q(a\v_1+b\v_2+c\v_3) = a^2+5b^2-117c^2\text{.}\) For the purposes of understanding \(Q\) this would be a much better basis. By choosing a different basis \(D'=\set{\v_1,\v_2,\v_3}\) we changed the matrix of \(B\) to
\begin{equation*} [B]_{D'}= \begin{bmatrix} 1&0&0\\0&5&0\\0&0&-117 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
With respect to the basis \(D''=\set{\v_1,\v_2/\sqrt5,\v_3/\sqrt{117}}\) we have the matrix
\begin{equation*} [B]_{D''}= \begin{bmatrix} 1&0&0\\0&1&0\\0&0&-1 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
