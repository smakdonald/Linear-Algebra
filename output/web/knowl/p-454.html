Polynomial interpolation questions can be rephrased in terms of solutions of linear systems of equations. Write down a general polynomial of degree at most \(n-1\) as
\begin{equation*} f = a_0 + a_1 x+ \cdots + a_{n-1} x^{n-1}. \end{equation*}
Then we want \(f\) to satisfy the system of \(n\) equations \(f(x_i) = y_i\) (\(i=1,\ldots,n\)). Written out more explicitly, we need to find \(a_{n-1},\ldots,a_0\in \F\) such that
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} a_0 + a_1 x_1 \cdots + a_{n-1}x_1^{n-1} &= y_1\\ a_0 + a_1 x_2\cdots + a_{n-1}x_2^{n-1} &= y_2\\ &\vdots\\ a_0 + a_1 x_n \cdots + a_{n-1} x_{n-1}^{n-1} &= y_n \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
Alternately, in matrix form, we want to solve the following equation for \((a_0, \ldots, a_{n-1})\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & x_1 & \cdots & x_1^{n-1} \\ 1 & x_2 & \cdots & x_2^{n-1} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots\\ 1 & x_n & \cdots & x_{n}^{n-1} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} a_{0} \\a_1 \\ \vdots\\a_{n-1}\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix} y_1 \\y_{2}\\\vdots\\y_n\end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}
The matrix on the left is called a Vandermonde matrix.
