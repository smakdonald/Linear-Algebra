The standard approach to solving such a system, called Gaussian elimination, involves adding or subtracting the equations from one another, possible multiplying some by constants. For instance, we might start the solution process for this system by subtracting the first equation from the second (with the intention of eliminating \(x\) from the second equation):
\begin{equation*} \veceqn{x+y-2z=-5} \ - \ \veceqn{x+2y-3z = -7} \ =\ \veceqn{-y + z = 2} \end{equation*}
We eliminate \(x\) from the third equation also, by computing
\begin{equation*} \veceqn{2x+7y-2z = 8} \ -\ 2\,\veceqn{x+2y-3z = -7} = \veceqn{3y+4z = 22}, \end{equation*}
and we have made progress on solving our system by deducing that any solution to the original system must also satisfy
\begin{equation*} \systeme{-y+z = 2, 3y+4z = 22\rlap{.}} \end{equation*}
We continue the process by computing
\begin{equation*} \veceqn{3y+4z=22} \ +\ 3\,\veceqn{-y+z = 2} = \veceqn{7z=28}, \end{equation*}
and thereby deduce that \(z=4\text{.}\) We could demonstrate this fact quickly by just writing down the linear combination of our equations
\begin{equation*} -\frac57\,\veceqn{x+2y-3z = -7}\ +\ \frac37\, \veceqn{x+y-2z=-5} \ +\ \frac17 \, \veceqn{2x+7y-2z = 8} \ =\ \veceqn{z=4}. \end{equation*}
We certainly don't advocate this as a sensible way to write down the manual solution of systems of linear equations. This is the first of many examples where we want to form linear combinations of things that aren't just vectors in two or three dimensions. This leads us to the notion of a vector space.
