Consider first vectors \(T(\u),T(\v)\in T(V)\text{.}\) For \(\l,\mu\in \F\) we have
\begin{equation*} \l T(\u) + \mu T(\v) = T(\l \u+\mu \v) \in T(V), \end{equation*}
so \(T(V)\) is closed under taking linear combinations. For \(\ker(T)\) we have a criterion for testing membership rather than a way of generating vectors, so the proof looks a little different. Take \(\u,\v\in \ker(T)\) and \(\l,\mu\in \F\text{.}\) We would like to prove that \(\l\u+\mu\v\in \ker(T)\text{,}\) and to verify this we simply apply \(T\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} T(\l\u+\mu\v) = \l T(\u) + \mu T(\v) = \l \0 + \mu \0 = \0+\0 = \0. \end{equation*}
Thus \(\ker(T)\) is closed under taking linear combinations and we are done.
