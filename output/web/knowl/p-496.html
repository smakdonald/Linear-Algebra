\begin{equation*} \ip{\v_i,\v_j} = \begin{cases} 1 & i=j \\ 0 & i\not= j \end{cases}. \end{equation*}
Consider what \(f\) does to the \(\v_i\text{.}\) For some \(\mu_i\in \R\) we have \(f(\v_i) = \mu_i\text{.}\) Define \(\v_f = \sum_1^n\overline{\mu_i} \v_i\text{.}\) Since \(B\) is a basis we know that for any \(\w\in V\) there exist \(\l_i\in \F\) such that \(\w=\Slv\text{,}\) and therefore
\begin{equation*} f(\w) = f\left(\Slv\right) = \sum_1^n \l_i f(\v_i) = \sum_1^n \l_i \mu_i = \sum_1^n \l_i \ip{\v_i,\v_f} = \ip{\w,\v_f}. \end{equation*}
Suppose that vectors \(\v,\v'\) are both candidates to be \(\v_f\text{;}\) in other words for all \(\w\in V\) we have
\begin{equation*} f(\w) = \ip{\w,\v} = \ip{\w,\v'}. \end{equation*}
In particular \(\ip{\w,\v-\v'}=0\) for all \(\w\in V\text{.}\) Choosing \(\w=\v-\v'\) gives \(\norm{\v-\v'}=0\) and therefore \(\v=\v'\text{.}\)
