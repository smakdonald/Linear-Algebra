We prove the result by induction on \(\dim(V)\text{.}\) If \(\dim(V)\le 1\) then the result is trivial since all matrices are diagonal. If \(\dim(V)>1\) and \(B \equiv 0\) then we can pick any basis and our matrix will be the zero matrix, and in particular diagonal. Suppose then that \(\dim(V)>1\) and \(B\) is not identically \(0\text{.}\) Let \(Q\) be the associated quadratic form. If \(Q\) were identically \(0\) then so also \(B\) would be, so there exists \(\v\in V\) such that \(Q(\v)=B(\v,\v)=\l_1\neq 0\text{.}\) Define
\begin{equation*} U = \setof{\w\in V}{B(\v,\w)=0}. \end{equation*}
The bilinearity of \(B\) allows us to check that \(U\) is a subspace of \(V\text{.}\) In fact we have \(V = \linspan(\v) \dsum U\text{.}\) To prove this note first that if \(\w\in \linspan(\v) \cap U\) then \(\w=\l\v\) for some \(\l\) and also
\begin{equation*} B(\v,\w)= B(\v,\l\v) = \l B(\v,\v) = 0, \end{equation*}
which implies that \(\l=0\) (since \(B(\v,\v)\not=0\)). Now consider an arbitrary \(\w\in V\text{.}\) If we set \(\w'=\w- \left(B(\v,\w)/B(\v,\v)\right)\cdot\v\) and \(\l=B(\v,\w)/B(\v,\v)\) then
\begin{equation*} B(\v,\w') = B(\v,\w) - \left(B(\v,\w)/B(\v,\v) \right)B(\v,\l\v)=0, \end{equation*}
so \(\w'\in U\text{.}\) Thus \(\w= \l\v + \w' \in \linspan(\v)+U\text{.}\) Now we know that \(\dim(U) < n\text{,}\) so by induction there is a basis \(D'=\{\v_2,\ldots,\v_n\}\) of \(U\) with respect to which the matrix of \(B\) (restricted to \(U\)) is diagonal, say
\begin{equation*} [B\mid_U]_{D'}=\begin{bmatrix} \l_2 & 0 &\cdots & 0\\ 0 & \l_3 &\cdots & 0\\ \vdots & \vdots &\cdots & \vdots\\ 0 & 0 &\cdots & \l_n\\ \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
By we get that \(D = \set{\v} \cup D'\) is a basis for \(V\) with respect to which the matrix of \(B\) is
\begin{equation*} [B\mid_U]_{D'}=\begin{bmatrix} \l_1 & 0 & 0 &\cdots & 0\\ 0 & \l_2 & 0 & \cdots & 0\\ 0 & 0 & \l_3 & \cdots & 0\\ \vdots & \vdots &\cdots & \vdots\\ 0 & 0 & 0& \cdots & \l_n\\ \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
and the result is proved.
