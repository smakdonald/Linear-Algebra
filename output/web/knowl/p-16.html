Looking at [cross-reference to target(s) "fig:add" missing or not unique] we see that if we pick an axis the measure of “distance along that axis” is additive: the answer for the sum of two vectors is the sum of the answers for each individually. In other words, letting \(L\) be the axis, we have
\begin{equation*} (\text{distance of $\u$ along $L$})+(\text{distance of $\v$ along $L$}) = (\text{distance of $\u+\v$ along $L$}). \end{equation*}
It's more obvious that if we scale a vector by a factor of \(7\) say, then the distance along the axis scales by the same factor. So, if \(c\) is any real number, we have
\begin{equation*} c\,(\text{distance of $\u$ along $L$})=(\text{distance of $c\,\u$ along $L$}). \end{equation*}
Again, using a bit of trigonometry, we have that the length of \(\u\) in the direction of \(L\) is just the length of \(\u\) times \(\cos(\theta)\text{,}\) where \(\theta\) is the (non-obtuse) angle between \(\u\) and \(L\text{.}\)
