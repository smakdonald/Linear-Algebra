Why does this help? Because we can split our calculation into small calculations that are trivial to do. Let's pick two vectors of length \(1\text{,}\) one along the \(x\)-axis and one along the \(y\)-axis. The traditional names for these vectors are \(\e_1\) and \(\e_2\text{.}\) Now suppose that \(\u=(a, b)=a\e_1+b\e_2\) and \(\w=(c, d) = c\e_1+d\e_2\text{.}\) Then, using additivity, we have
\begin{equation*} \u\cdot \w = (a\e_1+b\e_2) \cdot (c\e_1+d\e_2) = ac \, \e_1\cdot\e_1+ ad \, \e_1\cdot\e_2 +bc\, \e_1\cdot\e_2 + bd \,\e_2\cdot \e_2. \end{equation*}
Every term in this final expression is easy to work out geometrically. All lengths are \(1\text{,}\) all angles are either \(0\) or \(90^\circ\text{.}\) We have, for instance \(\e_1 \cdot \e_1 = \abs{\e_1}\abs{\e_1} \cos(0) = 1\times1\times1=1\text{.}\) Similarly,
\begin{equation*} \e_1\cdot\e_2 = 1 \qquad \text{and} \qquad \e_1\cdot\e_2 = \e_2\cdot \e_1 = 0. \end{equation*}
Altogether we get \((a, b)\cdot(c, d) = ac+bd\text{.}\)
