Note that the equation \(ax+by+cz=d\) can be written using dot product as
\begin{equation*} (a,b,c)\cdot (x,y,z)=d. \end{equation*}
Let's consider a particular solution \((x_0,y_0,z_0)\) to the above equation. For example, if \(a\neq 0\) I could pick \((x_0,y_0,z_0)=(d/a,0,0)\) and similarly if \(b\neq0\) and \(c\neq 0\text{.}\) Then we have
\begin{equation*} (a,b,c)\cdot (x_0,y_0,z_0)=d \end{equation*}
and subtracting this from the above equation gives
\begin{equation*} (a,b,c)\cdot (x-x_0,y-y_0,z-z_0)=0. \end{equation*}
This means \((x-x_0,y-y_0,z-z_0)\) is perpendicular to \((a,b,c)\) (or 0). Thinking of \((x-x_0,y-y_0,z-z_0)\) as a step with head at \((x,y,z)\) and tail at \((x_0,y_0,z_0)\) we see it is perpendicular to \((a,b,c)\text{.}\) There is a unique plane that passes through the point \((x_0,y_0,z_0)\) and is perpendicular to \((a,b,c)\) and we have just seen that \((x,y,z)\) is a solution if and only if it lies in this plane.
