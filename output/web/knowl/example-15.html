Example 2.4.

The rational numbers \(\Q\) are a field, indeed they are a subfield of \(\R\) (simply meaning that \(\Q\of \R\) and the field operations for \(\Q\) are the same whether you think of elements of \(\Q\) as rational numbers or real numbers). This fact means that conditions A1-A2, M1-M2, and C1 are trivially satisfied, since they're true for real numbers, not just rationals. Since the special real numbers \(0,1\) are in fact in \(\Q\) we also get A3, M3, and C2 for free. This only leaves A4 and M4; given \(x\in \Q\) we know that there are real numbers \(-x\) and \(x\inv\) such that \(x+(-x)=0\) and \(xx\inv=1\text{,}\) but what we need to know is that there are rational numbers with these properties  1 . Fortunately it is that case that if \(x\) is rational then so are \(-x\) and \(x\inv\text{.}\) Thus \(\Q\) is a field.
